The real story

Why people keep getting this wrong

The line “I got 99 problems but a bitch ain’t one” didn’t start as a Jay-Z invention — and it also isn’t a “Jay-Z stole it” situation. The confusion mostly comes from clipped podcast moments and reposts that drop the context.

Origins

Where the phrase came from

In the story told by Ice-T, the phrase surfaced during a casual conversation with Brother Marquis (from 2 Live Crew). Marquis said a version of the line, and Ice-T immediately recognized it as a hook. With permission to run with it, Ice-T built a full record around that concept years earlier.

The key detail: the phrase existed before it became a mainstream hook — it was part of hip-hop’s spoken culture first.

Later

What Jay-Z did (and what he didn’t do)

Jay-Z later used the same phrase as a hook for his own track — but built a different song and a different message. His version isn’t Ice-T’s song rewritten; it’s a new record structured around separate themes like pressure, profiling, fame, and legal trouble.

Reusing a well-known phrase and transforming it into a new concept is a common pattern in hip-hop lineage.

Takeaway

Bottom line

This story is best understood as evolution, not theft: a phrase moves through conversation, becomes a record, and later gets reinterpreted into a different record by another artist. The “stole the song” angle is largely an internet misread of what was meant.
